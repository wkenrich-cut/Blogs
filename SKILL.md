### Blog Post (600–1,200 words)
Structure:
1. **Hook** — One or two sentences. A crisis scenario, a brutal stat, or a hard truth.
2. **Problem** — The reality of manual incident response. Be vivid. Use specifics.
3. **Shift** — Introduce the task-led, automated approach. What changes when you do this right.
4. **Solution** — How Cutover Respond addresses it. Feature → benefit → outcome.
5. **Proof** — Customer outcomes, AWS Resilience Competency, or MTTR data.
6. **CTA** — "See it for yourself: [cutover.com/book-a-demo](https://cutover.com/book-a-demo)"

**Blog metadata to include:**
- Suggested title (punchy, ideally a provocative question or bold statement)
- Meta description (1–2 sentences, outcome-focused)
- Suggested tags (e.g. Major Incident Management, MTTR, SRE, IT Resilience)

### Thought Leadership Article (800–1,500 words)
- Lead with a bold industry POV: "The era of manual incident response is over." "Your ITSM ticket isn't saving you during a P1."
- Build the argument before introducing Cutover — earn the conclusion.
- Speak to the reader as a peer, not a prospect.
- End with a forward-looking statement + soft CTA.
- Suitable for LinkedIn Articles, trade publications (ITPro, InfoSecurity), company blog.

### LinkedIn Post (150–300 words)

**The Visual Rhythm Rule — apply to every LinkedIn post without exception.**

LinkedIn posts live or die on visual rhythm. Before finalising any post, mentally squint at it. The line-length "blocks" must vary like music — not march like soldiers. The pattern to aim for:

> Short. Then a longer line that explains, gives context, and pulls the reader forward because it feels human. Short again. Medium thought that lands the idea. One word. Longer sentence that earns the payoff and leaves them thinking. Short.

If all the blocks look the same size — rewrite. The eye needs contrast to keep moving.

**Rules:**
- Never stack two long lines back to back
- Never stack more than two short lines in a row without a longer one
- Every post should have at least one single-short-punchy line standing alone
- Vary paragraph length: one sentence, then maybe three, then one, then two — never uniform
- Write like a human typed it in a moment of clarity, not like a content team approved it

**Line 1 = the hook.** Provocative question, brutal stat, or hard truth. Must earn the scroll-stop.

End with a question to drive comments OR a soft CTA to the product page — never both.

3–5 hashtags: #MajorIncidentManagement #MTTR #SRE #ITResilience #Cutover

Always offer **2–3 variations** with different angles (e.g. one for MIMs, one for C-suite).

## Content Writing Guidelines

### Voice & Tone: Punchy & Bold
- **Lead with a punch.** Every piece opens with a line that demands attention — a stat, a provocation, a scenario.
- **Short sentences. Hard stops. Whitespace.** Don't bury the point.
- **Be direct.** Say "Manual incident response is broken" not "There are challenges in traditional incident response approaches."
- **Outcome-first.** Anchor everything to business impact — MTTR, cost of downtime, regulatory risk, team burnout.
- **Confident, not corporate.** No hedge words. State things.
- **Active voice always.** "Cutover Respond mobilises your team in seconds" not "Teams can be mobilised more quickly."

### Key Messages to Reinforce
1. **Agentic Resilience** — Systems are now too complex for humans to manage alone. AI agents aren't a nice-to-have; they're the only way to survive the next cascading failure. Cutover Respond is built for this reality.
2. **Runbook-Ops beats Ticket-Ops and Chat-Ops** — Every competitor makes you talk about or log the problem. Cutover Respond solves it. *"Incident.io is for talking about a problem. ServiceNow is for logging it. Cutover is for solving it."*
3. **Audit-Ready, not Slack History** — When your regulator or legal team asks for the incident record, a Slack export is not an answer. Cutover's indelible audit trail is a byproduct of execution, not a reconstruction.
4. **The margin for error is shrinking** — More AI agents, more SaaS, more cyber and geopolitical threats. The 2015 playbook (chat + tickets) is being run in a 2026 threat environment. That gap is now dangerous.
5. **28–50% faster MTTR** — Not a claim, a verified outcome compared to traditional chat-based response.
6. Every incident becomes a learning opportunity — AI linked to MTTR reward function improves with every run.

### Terms to Use
- "Major Incident Manager (MIM)" — the person running the incident, not just a ticket
- "Resolver" — the technical person executing tasks
- "MTTR" (Mean Time to Resolution) — always spell out on first use
- "Runbook" — the automated task playbook triggered during an incident
- "Task-led approach" — Cutover's differentiated methodology

### Terms to Avoid
- Don't position Respond as just "another ITSM tool"
- Avoid "simple" or "easy" — incidents are complex; Cutover makes them *manageable*
- Don't say "AI-powered" generically — be specific (AI agents, automated triage, post-incident AI learning)
