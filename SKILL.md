## About Cutover Respond

Cutover Respond is a major incident management platform that reduces Mean Time to Resolution (MTTR) through:

- **Rapid team mobilization** — Automates the process of engaging Resolvers and Major Incident Managers (MIMs), eliminating manual admin work so teams can focus on resolution.
- **Task-led approach** — Turns disconnected incident response activities into a clear, assigned task list with full accountability and visibility.
- **Real-time collaboration** — Executives, MIMs, and Resolvers all self-serve live status updates in one place. No status-report interruptions.
- **AI agents and automation** — Automates routine tasks (log checks, health checks, notifications, triage, documentation). AI agents provide insights and recommendations to accelerate response.
- **Post-incident learning** — Automated, indelible audit trail satisfies regulators and internal review. AI links to MTTR reward function to improve response with every incident.

### Key differentiators vs. competitors
- **vs. PagerDuty**: PagerDuty focuses on alerting; Cutover Respond is the orchestration and execution layer for the full incident lifecycle.
- **vs. ServiceNow**: ServiceNow is the system of record/ticketing. Cutover Respond is the execution engine that integrates with ServiceNow to bridge the gap between ticket and resolution.
- Cutover is an **AWS Resilience Software Competency** partner — one of the few inaugural AWS partners with this recognition.

### ServiceNow MIM — specific competitive detail (use in FSI/sales-oriented content)

ServiceNow's Major Incident Management (MIM) module is the incumbent tool at most large FSI accounts. Clients describe it as functional for administering an incident, but fundamentally broken for *resolving* one. Specific pain points reported directly by customers:

- **Wrong starting point** — SNow MIM assumes an incident begins with raising a ticket. For a true major incident, you need the right people on the problem immediately — not filling in forms.
- **Tasks lost in chat** — Work gets buried in channels, making it nearly impossible to track who is doing what or reduce time to resolution.
- **Status reporting steals resolution time** — Because visibility is so poor, members of the team actively fixing the incident have to stop and reconstruct status to brief stakeholders.
- **Forensic audit is manual and slow** — Post-incident, teams up to MD level are consumed rebuilding the timeline. This is time that should be spent on the next incident, not the last one.
- **Groundhog Day problem** — Teams manually log 5 or so tasks into a ticket post-incident. This rarely reflects what actually happened, so the next incident starts from scratch. No learning, no compounding improvement.
- **Poor UI, poor design** — SNow MIM's interface doesn't help users solve the incident. Nothing in its workflow is oriented around speed. In reality, MTTR is the only metric that matters.
- **Dynamic task model gap** — SNow MIM struggles to support a more dynamic task structure and makes it harder to bring AI agents into workflows.

**The SNow MIM one-liner:** *"ServiceNow logs the incident. Cutover Respond resolves it."*

**FSI origin story (use to establish credibility in financial services content):** Cutover spent a decade partnering with the world's largest financial institutions on IT Disaster Recovery. Those clients asked us to apply the same principles to incident management — because they were running their DR in a manual format with the same underlying problems: no orchestration, no visibility, no audit trail. Respond was built from that demand.

**The DR-to-Respond bridge (use when writing for FSI audiences):** Cutover demonstrated ~53% faster recovery times with DR clients. Those same clients then asked about incident management. ### The competitive position in one frame

Every competitor is built on **Ticket-Ops** (ServiceNow) or **Chat-Ops** (PagerDuty, Incident.io, FireHydrant). Neither can orchestrate a complex, multi-team incident response. You can communicate in a ticket or chat thread. You can log. But you cannot *execute* with clarity, sequence, accountability, and a real-time audit trail in those interfaces.

**Cutover Respond is Runbook-Ops.** A live execution environment — not a document, not a chat thread — where every task has an owner, a sequence, a timestamp, and a status. All participants in the same operational space. Indelible audit trail as a byproduct of execution. AI agents operating inside the layer, not alongside it.

This gap is widening. Rising tech complexity (AI agents, multi-SaaS, hybrid cloud), more frequent and severe incidents (cyber, geopolitical, regulatory), and a lower margin for error mean organisations running Ticket-Ops or Chat-Ops are using a 2015 playbook in a 2026 threat environment.

## Content Writing Guidelines

### Voice & Tone: Punchy & Bold
- **Lead with a punch.** Every piece opens with a line that demands attention — a stat, a provocation, a scenario.
- **Short sentences. Hard stops. Whitespace.** Don't bury the point.
- **Be direct.** Say "Manual incident response is broken" not "There are challenges in traditional incident response approaches."
- **Outcome-first.** Anchor everything to business impact — MTTR, cost of downtime, regulatory risk, team burnout.
- **Confident, not corporate.** No hedge words. State things.
- **Active voice always.** "Cutover Respond mobilises your team in seconds" not "Teams can be mobilised more quickly."

### Key Messages to Reinforce
1. **Agentic Resilience** — Systems are now too complex for humans to manage alone. AI agents aren't a nice-to-have; they're the only way to survive the next cascading failure. Cutover Respond is built for this reality.
2. **Runbook-Ops beats Ticket-Ops and Chat-Ops** — Every competitor makes you talk about or log the problem. Cutover Respond solves it. *"Incident.io is for talking about a problem. ServiceNow is for logging it. Cutover is for solving it."*
3. **Audit-Ready, not Slack History** — When your regulator or legal team asks for the incident record, a Slack export is not an answer. Cutover's indelible audit trail is a byproduct of execution, not a reconstruction.
4. **The margin for error is shrinking** — More AI agents, more SaaS, more cyber and geopolitical threats. The 2015 playbook (chat + tickets) is being run in a 2026 threat environment. That gap is now dangerous.
5. **28–50% faster MTTR** — Not a claim, a verified outcome compared to traditional chat-based response.
6. Every incident becomes a learning opportunity — AI linked to MTTR reward function improves with every run.

### Terms to Use
- "Major Incident Manager (MIM)" — the person running the incident, not just a ticket
- "Resolver" — the technical person executing tasks
- "MTTR" (Mean Time to Resolution) — always spell out on first use
- "Runbook" — the automated task playbook triggered during an incident
- "Task-led approach" — Cutover's differentiated methodology

### Terms to Avoid
- Don't position Respond as just "another ITSM tool"
- Avoid "simple" or "easy" — incidents are complex; Cutover makes them *manageable*
- Don't say "AI-powered" generically — be specific (AI agents, automated triage, post-incident AI learning)

### LinkedIn Post (150–300 words)

**The Visual Rhythm Rule — apply to every LinkedIn post without exception.**

LinkedIn posts live or die on visual rhythm. Before finalising any post, mentally squint at it. The line-length "blocks" must vary like music — not march like soldiers. The pattern to aim for:

> Short. Then a longer line that explains, gives context, and pulls the reader forward because it feels human. Short again. Medium thought that lands the idea. One word. Longer sentence that earns the payoff and leaves them thinking. Short.

If all the blocks look the same size — rewrite. The eye needs contrast to keep moving.

**Rules:**
- Never stack two long lines back to back
- Never stack more than two short lines in a row without a longer one
- Every post should have at least one single-short-punchy line standing alone
- Vary paragraph length: one sentence, then maybe three, then one, then two — never uniform
- Write like a human typed it in a moment of clarity, not like a content team approved it

**Line 1 = the hook.** Provocative question, brutal stat, or hard truth. Must earn the scroll-stop.

End with a question to drive comments OR a soft CTA to the product page — never both.

3–5 hashtags: #MajorIncidentManagement #MTTR #SRE #ITResilience #Cutover

Always offer **2–3 variations** with different angles (e.g. one for MIMs, one for C-suite).
